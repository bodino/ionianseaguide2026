**Winds**: April in the Ionian averages **6–15 kn, variable direction** — the Maïstro NW pattern of summer isn't yet established. Expect some days of sailing, some of motoring. Occasional 20+ kn gusts through the channels.

## 1. The Boat — Bavaria Cruiser 45 (2019)

The Bavaria Cruiser 45 (designed by Farr Yacht Design, built in Giebelstadt, Germany) is a mass-market charter workhorse: beamy, stable, easy to sail short-handed, and forgiving. A 2019 hull is in the late run of this line (replaced by the C45 Style / C46).

### Key Specifications


| Spec                          | Value                     | Why it matters                                                            |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| LOA                           | ~13.99 m (45'11")         | Fits standard Ionian quays; check slot width before committing            |
| LWL                           | ~12.60 m                  | Hull speed ≈ 8.6 kn under ideal conditions                                |
| Beam                          | ~4.35 m                   | Very wide aft — huge cockpit, but watch finger-pier clearance             |
| Draft (standard)              | ~2.10 m                   | Shallow-draft option: ~1.65 m. **Check yours** — matters for Ionian coves |
| Displacement                  | ~11.2 t                   | Heavy-ish; she carries way and takes a moment to stop                     |
| Ballast                       | ~3.1 t (lead keel)        | Stiff in a blow, comfortable at ~20° heel                                 |
| Mast height (above WL)        | ~20.5 m                   | Check bridge/cable clearances (Lefkada canal is fine)                     |
| Sail area (main + 106% genoa) | ~100 m²                   | In-mast furling main is standard on charter boats                         |
| Engine                        | Volvo Penta D2-55 (55 hp) | Saildrive, 3-blade folding or fixed prop                                  |
| Fuel                          | ~210 L diesel             | ~40–50 hrs motoring range at cruise RPM                                   |
| Water                         | ~360–530 L (2 tanks)      | Plan on 2 showers/day max for 6 people                                    |
| Holding tanks                 | 2 × ~80 L                 | Legally required inside harbours; discharge offshore                      |
| Berths                        | 8–10 in 3 or 4 cabins     | Charter version usually 4-cabin, 4-head                                   |


### Hull Character

- **Wide transom, twin rudders** on some configs (single rudder on most charter C45s). Twin rudders grip hard when heeled; single rudder needs less heel angle to bite.
- **Hard chines aft** give form stability — she sits flat, doesn't round up easily.
- **Sail-drive transmission** (not shaft): means the leg goes through the hull with a rubber diaphragm. **Check the diaphragm service date** — if older than 7 years it's out of class.

### Typical Charter Config (what you'll likely have)

- 4 double cabins, 4 heads with separate showers
- In-mast furling mainsail, furling genoa (105–108%)
- Bow thruster (most 2019 charter C45s — check at handover)
- Electric windlass with foot-switches and helm remote
- Dual helms with Raymarine plotters
- Bimini + sprayhood, cockpit table
- Swim platform that folds down (check locking pin at handover)

## 2. Deck Layout & Rigging

![Bavaria C45 deck layout — bird's-eye view](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/bodino/ionianseaguide2026/main/diagrams/deck-layout.png)

### Standing Rigging (the wires that hold the mast up)

- **Forestay**: carries the genoa furler.
- **Backstay**: from masthead to stern. On the C45 it's often split (two legs meeting at a T) so you can walk under it.
- **Cap shrouds** (upper) → masthead. **Lowers** → spreaders. **Diagonal intermediates** on the in-mast version.
- **Check at handover**: no "meat-hooks" (broken strands), clevis pins moused with seizing wire, rig tension roughly even side-to-side.

### Running Rigging (the ropes you actually pull)

All lines lead aft through deck organisers to clutches either side of the companionway, then to secondary winches.


| Line                        | What it does                                                | Typical side                       |
| --------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Main halyard                | Raises the mainsail (stays hoisted — you furl, not drop)    | Port                               |
| Main outhaul / furling line | Rolls main in/out of the mast                               | Starboard (often at mast base too) |
| Genoa furling line          | Rolls genoa around forestay                                 | Starboard, leads to cockpit        |
| Genoa sheets (port & stbd)  | Trim the genoa                                              | Primary winches aft                |
| Mainsheet                   | Trims main, on a coach-roof traveller or German main system | Centre/above companionway          |
| Boom vang (kicker)          | Pulls boom down; shapes main in light air                   | Either side                        |
| Reef lines                  | Not used with in-mast furling — you just roll partially     |                                    |


### Winch Discipline

- **Always** three turns minimum before loading; add the fourth under load.
- **Never** put your hand between rope and drum under tension — digits get removed.
- **Load the tailer, not the grinder.** One person tails (pulls the loose end taut), another grinds.
- **Self-tailers on the C45**: drop the line into the jaws with the lay going the right way (check the arrow on the jaw cap).

## 3. Sail Handling

### Mainsail — In-mast furling

The mainsail lives rolled up inside a slot in the mast. You deploy it by easing the furling line while pulling the outhaul.

**Deploying (unfurling) the main:**

1. **Head into the wind** — within ~20° either side. This is non-negotiable with in-mast; off the wind the sail jams.
2. Ease the main sheet and vang fully — boom must swing free.
3. Release the furling-line clutch.
4. Pull the outhaul (via the winch, 3–4 turns) steadily. Watch the sail come out. The furling line should feed itself out of its clutch; don't let it birds-nest.
5. Stop at the desired reef point (battens or marks on the outhaul line tell you how much is out).
6. Cleat both lines. Bear away to sailing angle. Sheet in.

**Reefing / furling in:**

1. Head back up into the wind. Ease the sheet and vang.
2. Keep tension on the outhaul (via the winch, easing slowly).
3. Pull in the furling line hand-over-hand — it should be easy if the main is luffing. **If it's hard, stop.** You're probably not head-to-wind, or the sail is loaded. Resistance = future jam.
4. Finish with the sail fully rolled, or partial reef at a marked point.

**In-mast furling pitfalls:**

- **Never furl with the boom loaded off-centre** — the sail twists and jams half-in the mast. Recovering a jammed in-mast furler offshore is miserable; you may have to go up the mast.
- **Don't over-tension the outhaul** when fully deployed; stretched sailcloth doesn't re-furl cleanly.
- **Keep the topping lift / rigid vang set** so the boom stays horizontal during furling — sail must enter the slot at 90°.

### Genoa — Roller furling on the forestay

Simpler. Works from any point of sail, though easier when not heavily loaded.

**Deploy:** Release furling-line clutch, pull on the leeward sheet via the primary winch. As the sail unrolls, the furling line should run out cleanly — tend it so it doesn't tangle on the drum.

**Reef:** Ease the working sheet (don't let it fly — you want the sail to roll cleanly, not flog). Pull the furling line. A few extra turns around the forestay = reef.

**Sheet-car position:** The genoa sheet leads through a car on a track. Move the car **forward** when reefed (smaller sail, lower clew, leech closes). Move **aft** for full genoa or when the leech is too tight.

### Points of Sail — Quick Reference

![Points of sail — sectors relative to true wind](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/bodino/ionianseaguide2026/main/diagrams/points-of-sail.png)

- Close-hauled = ~30–45° off the wind. Boat heels most. Flattest sails, vang on, genoa cars aft.
- Beam reach = wind on the beam (90°). Fastest point of sail, most comfortable.
- Broad reach = ~120–150°. Ease everything. Watch the boom.
- Run = ~180°. Unstable; wind can catch the back of the main and fling the boom across (crash gybe). **Rig a preventer** (a line from the boom end forward to a bow cleat) when running.

### Gybing vs Tacking

- **Tack**: turn bow through the wind. Sails flip sides easily, no drama on a cruiser.
- **Gybe**: turn stern through the wind. Boom crosses the cockpit hard. **Procedure**: sheet the main in tight to centre BEFORE the gybe, turn through, ease out on the new side. Call "Preparing to gybe — heads!" and get the crew low.

## 4. Engine & Mechanical Systems

### Volvo Penta D2-55 (the engine)

A 4-cylinder naturally-aspirated marine diesel. Reliable, simple, parts everywhere in Greece.

**Pre-start checks (every single day):**

![Daily engine pre-start checks](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/bodino/ionianseaguide2026/main/diagrams/engine-checks.png)

**Starting procedure:**

1. Gear lever in neutral (centre detent).
2. Turn key to "on" — glow-plug warning illuminates; wait ~10 sec in cool weather.
3. Turn to start, release as it catches. Don't crank more than 10 sec.
4. Check: oil pressure lamp off, alternator lamp off, water from exhaust.

**Shutdown:**

1. Neutral.
2. Let idle 1–2 min (turbo isn't fitted on D2-55 but letting temps even out is still good).
3. Pull the stop knob (red) or push stop button; key off.
4. **Close the raw-water seacock** if leaving boat overnight unattended (belt-and-braces).

### Throttle / Gear Lever

Single-lever control at each helm.

- **Centre detent** = neutral. Feel for the click.
- **Forward / back** past the detent = gear engaged at idle.
- **Further** = throttle up in that gear.
- Gear engagement is hydraulic; shift *at idle* or you'll shock-load the saildrive.

**Cruise RPM**: ~~2200 rpm gives ~6.5–7 kn and sips fuel (~~3 L/hr). Max continuous ~2800. Don't baby it — run it hard for a few minutes daily to burn off carbon.

### Prop Walk

A single right-hand prop walks the stern **to port in reverse**. The C45 is no exception. Use this for Med mooring: initial reverse kicks your stern left. Give a short burst first to pre-position, then apply steady reverse with rudder centred.

### Saildrive

The leg through the hull. Maintenance concerns:

- **Rubber diaphragm** (the seal where the leg meets the hull): replace every 7 years. Check service date at handover.
- **Zinc anode** on the prop: lost anodes mean galvanic corrosion of the leg — expensive.
- **Folding prop**: opens under power, folds for sailing. If it doesn't open (weed, line), you lose propulsion. **Back down in reverse** briefly before arriving at a dock to clear the blades.

### Fuel System

- Tank under the aft cabin floor typically, vented to a deck vent on the hull side.
- **Fuel filter** (primary) is a Racor with a clear bowl — look for water at the bottom (water is denser and sits low). Drain the petcock if you see it.
- **Secondary filter** is on the engine itself.
- Greek diesel is generally clean but tank-bottom gunk gets stirred up in a chop. If the engine hunts or dies, change or bleed the filter.

### Stern Gland / Saildrive — What you should *not* see

- Water dripping steadily from the saildrive bellows (bilge rising) → call the charter base immediately.
- Black oil film in the bilge under the engine → oil leak, find the source before running further.

## 5. Electrical, Water, Gas & Heads

### 12 V / 24 V / 230 V architecture

The C45 runs on a 12 V DC system from a battery bank, plus 230 V AC when plugged in shore-side.

![12 V / 230 V electrical architecture](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/bodino/ionianseaguide2026/main/diagrams/electrical.png)

*Windlass runs through its own solenoid — huge current draw, direct from the service bank via the battery switch.*

### Battery Management

- **Two banks**: engine-start (small, never discharge below 80%), service/house (bigger, powers everything else).
- **Main battery switch** is usually in the aft cabin or cockpit locker. Positions: OFF / 1 / 2 / BOTH. Normal: each on its own setting. Use BOTH only as an emergency combine (e.g., start battery flat).
- **Don't let the service bank drop below 11.8 V resting** — lead-acid damage accumulates.
- **Fridge is the #1 drain**. If anchored 24 hrs without running the engine, expect to need engine or shore power to recharge.
- **Run the engine 1 hr/day at cruise RPM** if on anchor, just to keep the bank alive. This also heats water.

### Fresh Water

- 2 tanks (often one under each saloon settee). Total ~360–530 L depending on model.
- Electric pressure pump turns on with the panel. Accumulator tank smooths the flow.
- **If a tap runs air**, the tank is empty or a valve is closed. **Turn the pump off immediately** — running dry burns it out in minutes.
- **Hot water**: calorifier is heated by (a) engine coolant loop while motoring, or (b) 230 V element when plugged in. No engine + no shore power = cold showers.
- **Ration**: with 4+ people, assume 60–80 L/person/day if casual. Plan refills every 2–3 days. Most Ionian harbours have water on the quay (sometimes paid, sometimes free).

### Gas (LPG) — Galley stove

- Bottle lives in a sealed, self-draining locker aft.
- **Solenoid switch** at the nav station: must be on to have gas at the stove.
- **Always close the solenoid** (and ideally the bottle valve) before leaving the boat or sleeping.
- Smell gas = open companionway and cockpit lockers, don't operate any switch, turn off bottle at source, ventilate. LPG is heavier than air; it pools in the bilge.

### Heads (Toilets)

Two kinds you'll encounter on a 2019 C45:

**Manual (Jabsco-style) pump heads:**

- Valve lever to "flush/wet" position: seawater in as you pump.
- Use: 4–5 pumps flush, then switch to "dry", 10–15 pumps to clear the hose to holding/overboard.
- **Rule**: nothing in the bowl unless you've eaten it (plus a modest amount of thin paper). Wet wipes, tampons, kitchen roll — instant block.

**Electric heads** (more common on 2019 chartering):

- Button flushes. Still honour the rule above.
- The macerator can hear a coin hit the bowl from three cabins away. Same rule.

**Holding tank vs overboard:**

- Inside harbours and within 3 nm of shore in protected zones: **holding tank only** (Greek law).
- Y-valve under the head sink switches between overboard and tank. Charter bases often lock it to tank.
- Empty the tank offshore (pump-out or gravity drain), well away from anchorages and swimming.

## 6. Navigation & Electronics

Typical 2019 C45 charter electronics: Raymarine Axiom or eS-series chartplotter at each helm, wind/speed/depth instruments, VHF with DSC, autopilot, and AIS receive (sometimes transponder).

### What you need from the plotter, minimum

1. **COG / SOG** (course & speed over ground) — what the boat is actually doing, as GPS sees it.
2. **HDG** (heading) — where the bow points.
3. **Depth** — metres under the keel or under transducer (know which!).
4. **AWS / AWA** — apparent wind speed/angle.
5. **TWS / TWA** — true wind (computed) — this is what the forecast talks about.
6. **Waypoint / route** — route function for multi-leg passages.
7. **AIS** — see other vessels' name, course, CPA (closest point of approach).

### VHF Radio

- **Channel 16** — international calling and distress. Monitor it when underway.
- **Channel 13** — bridge-to-bridge (commercial). Lefkada floating bridge uses 12 locally.
- **Channel 9** — marinas and some harbour traffic in Greece.
- **DSC distress button** — flip the red cover, hold 5 sec. Sends your MMSI and GPS position automatically. Only use for genuine grave and imminent danger.
- **Your MMSI** is specific to the boat — note it down at handover.

### The Lefkada Floating Bridge

A swing bridge at the north end of the Lefkada Canal that opens **on the hour**, every hour, typically 07:00–23:00 (check at the base — schedules change seasonally). Queue upstream 10 min before; go through when the traffic light turns green. VHF 12. It is tight and the current sets you sideways in strong northerly wind — helm with intent.

### Paper Backup

Carry an Imray G12 Ionian chart (or equivalent). Plotter failures happen; knowing your position on paper with a compass bearing is the lowest-cost insurance you have.

## 7. Boat Handling — Docking, Anchoring, Med Mooring

The Ionian will test three specific manoeuvres more than anything else:

1. Med mooring stern-to a quay (daily)
2. Anchoring in a rocky/weedy bay (daily)
3. Long-line ashore to a taverna's private pontoon or a cove tree (frequent)

### Med Mooring — Stern-to

This is how you dock in Greece 90% of the time. Boats line up like books on a shelf, stern to the quay, anchor dropped ahead.

![Med mooring stern-to — 3-step approach](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/bodino/ionianseaguide2026/main/diagrams/med-mooring.png)

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Scout**: motor slowly past your slot. Note depth, wind direction, neighbours' anchor chains, and which side has less crosswind.
2. **Brief the crew**: who's on the bow (anchor/windlass), who handles stern lines (2 lines minimum: port and starboard quarter), who fends off.
3. **Position**: 4–5 boat lengths out from the quay, perpendicular. Stop. Engage reverse briefly to kill headway.
4. **Drop anchor** at that point. Pay out chain as you start to reverse.
5. **Reverse straight back** — this is the hard part. Use short bursts of forward to correct if the stern walks off-line. Bow thruster for fine adjustments.
6. **Chain scope**: aim for 4× depth in calm conditions, 5× if wind is building. In 6 m depth, 25–30 m out.
7. **Arrive** 1–2 m from the quay. Crew steps OFF (not jumps) with two lines, one to each of your aft cleats, crossed to far bollards on the quay for geometry.
8. **Tension up**: windlass takes in chain to pull bow out from quay, stern lines set the distance to the quay. 50 cm off the quay is safe for boarding; further is better if swell is expected.

**Common mistakes:**

- **Anchoring too close** — your anchor ends up under another boat's keel or tangled with theirs. Drop further out.
- **Reversing with the wind on the beam** — the bow blows off fast on a wide-beam cruiser. Counter with the bow thruster or short forward bursts.
- **Going too slow in reverse** — no steerage. Give it enough reverse to have water flowing past the rudder. The C45 needs ~2.5 kn in reverse to steer cleanly.
- **Ignoring prop walk** — expect the stern to kick to port. Start your approach aimed 10° to starboard of your slot.

### Anchoring in a Bay

**Choose**:

- Sand (holds best — light patches on the chart/bottom when you look down).
- Not weed (dark patches — Posidonia meadow; anchor skates).
- Not rock (anchor jams; environmentally protected in many places).

**Scope**:

- 5:1 minimum (chain length = 5× depth including freeboard to bow roller, ~1.5 m).
- 7:1 in forecast blow.
- All-chain rode, so less scope works than with rope/chain.

**Procedure**:

1. Motor into the bay slowly. Eyeball the bottom — sunlight + polarised sunglasses essential.
2. Pick your spot, accounting for swing circle (where you'll sit after you drift back).
3. Nose into the wind, stop, reverse gently to start drifting back.
4. Drop anchor — chain goes out controlled, not free-fall.
5. At full scope, snub with engine at ~1500 RPM reverse for 20–30 sec. If the boat stops and holds, anchor is set. If you drag, reset.
6. **Check with a transit**: pick two shore features lined up and watch — if they separate, you're moving.
7. **Anchor alarm** on the plotter: set it to your swing radius + 10 m.

### Long-line Ashore

Common in the Ionian because bays are deep close to shore. You anchor in deep water and run a long line from the stern to a rock or tree on the beach.

Procedure: anchor as above, reversing toward shore. Swimmer or dinghy takes the long line ashore (150 m floating line on a reel in the lazarette). Secures to a rock with a round turn and two half hitches, or a purpose-installed ring if the bay has one. Tension up.

Pros: no swinging, fit more boats in.
Cons: side-on to any swell — uncomfortable if the wind shifts.

## 8. The Ionian — Geography, Weather, Culture

### Geography

![Ionian sailing area & your 7-day route](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/bodino/ionianseaguide2026/main/diagrams/ionian-route.png)

- **Lefkada** is the northernmost of the Ionian charter bases. Just south of the canal you're already in sheltered waters.
- The core charter cruising ground is the **Inner Ionian Sea** — the water between Lefkada, the mainland, Meganisi, Kalamos, Kastos, and Ithaca. Short hops (10–25 nm), many anchorages.
- **Kefalonia** and especially **Zakynthos** are bigger jumps (30–40 nm one way), more open water, less-sheltered anchorages. Feasible in a fortnight if weather cooperates.

### Weather — The Maïstro

The defining Ionian wind is the **Maïstro** (also Maestro): a thermal NW wind that builds through the day in summer.

**Typical summer day:**


| Time        | Wind                                                    |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| 06:00–10:00 | Calm to light variable (<5 kn)                          |
| 10:00–13:00 | Maïstro fills in from NW, builds to 10–15 kn            |
| 13:00–18:00 | Maïstro peak, 15–22 kn, sometimes 25 kn in the channels |
| 18:00–21:00 | Dies down to <10 kn                                     |
| 21:00–06:00 | Calm, occasional land breeze                            |


Implication: **sail in the afternoon, dock by 15:00–16:00** to get a good spot before the harbours fill and before the wind peaks during your mooring manoeuvre.

### Other Weather

- **Thunderstorms**: occasional in shoulder seasons (May, September). Build on the mountains, roll off at dusk. Look for towering cumulonimbus; be under shelter before they arrive.
- **Sirocco / Ostria** (southerly): rarer in summer. Brings humidity and can kick up swell in south-facing bays. Check the forecast daily.
- **Bora / Gregale**: rare in summer but in spring/autumn a NE katabatic can come down the mountains hard and fast. Flat water to whitecaps in 30 min.
- **Forecasts**: use Poseidon (Hellenic National Weather Service), Windy (ECMWF model), Windfinder. Cross-reference at least two. VHF forecast on Ch 16 → directed channel.

### Cultural Notes

- **Tavernas and the quay**: many tavernas own or manage a stretch of town quay. You may berth for free in exchange for dining with them. Standard, not a scam — but confirm terms before tying up.
- **Harbour dues**: most harbours have a collector who comes round in the evening. €10–25/night typical depending on port and facilities. Port police in major harbours (Vathi, Fiskardo) will want to see your papers.
- **Siesta**: 14:00–17:00 most shops close. Plan provisioning in the morning.
- **Tipping**: not expected at tavernas, but round up. Fuel dock attendants appreciate a couple of euros.

### 10–14 day extensions

### Golden Rules

- **Always have a Plan B harbour** within 2 hrs sail if the weather turns.
- **Don't commit to a downwind run** you can't motor back from.
- **West coasts are exposed to the Maïstro swell**; east coasts of islands are typically lee shores in summer.
- **Fuel and water**: top up in Nidri, Vathi, Fiskardo, Sami — not every village has a fuel dock.

## 11. Hazards & Local Knowledge

### Things that will ruin your day

1. **Weed on the anchor**: Posidonia seagrass wraps anchors and stops them setting. If you drag once, lift fully, clear the fluke, move to a sandy patch. Don't just "try again" without a visual.
2. **Other boats' anchor chains**: Med-moored harbours have crossed chains everywhere. If on departure your anchor lifts someone else's, don't just drive forward — you'll drag them off the quay. Stop, lift them gently to the surface, pass a line under their chain, drop yours, unhook yours from under theirs, retrieve your line. Diver in swim-shorts works too.
3. **The Lefkada Canal chop**: Short, steep, confused chop when wind is against current in the canal. Annoying, not dangerous — slow down.
4. **Ferry wash**: Big ferries between Lefkada, Meganisi, Ithaca, and Kefalonia throw significant wake. Turn into the waves, throttle to idle.
5. **Rocks off headlands**: Many Ionian headlands have unmarked rocks 50–100 m off. Stay 200 m off any point unless you've got a known track.
6. **Mooring line chafe at the quay**: concrete quays chew through lines overnight. Chafe gear or a piece of hose slipped over the line at the bollard.
7. **Stern-to and bathing ladder**: the folding swim platform is your gangway — keep it clear and the lock-pin engaged when underway.
8. **Gas-bottle left on**: discussed above. Bilge explosions are rare because people do close bottles. Don't be the exception.

### West-coast passages

If you plan to sail up the west coast of Lefkada (e.g., to Paxos), be aware it's fully exposed to the Maïstro and has few bail-out harbours. Go early morning, stay offshore of the reefs at Cape Doucato, and watch the weather like a hawk.

### Swell wrapping into anchorages

"Protected" east-coast anchorages are protected from the *wind* but long-period swell from a distant weather system wraps around headlands and makes roll-y nights. If your anchorage opens east and there's been a big NW blow, expect some roll. Stern-to the swell if possible.

## 12. Safety, Emergencies & Comms

### Required safety kit (charter boats are inspected, but verify)

- Lifejackets for all crew, one each (test inflators if auto).
- Harnesses + tethers (jackstays run along the deck — clip on when going forward at night or in weather).
- Dan buoy + horseshoe lifebuoy on the stern pulpit.
- Liferaft in a cradle on the coachroof or in a locker — HRU or painter-activated.
- Flares: 2 parachute red, 2 hand-held red, 2 orange smoke minimum.
- Fire extinguishers: galley, each cabin, engine room. Know the engine one.
- First aid kit, seasickness meds.
- Engine cut-off lanyard (if fitted to this helm).
- Bolt cutters to cut rigging if dismasted (usually in a lazarette).
- Emergency tiller (check where it stows — autopilot + wheel failure is rare but possible).

### Man Overboard (MOB)

Call it out: "**MAN OVERBOARD**". Then:

1. Hit the MOB button on the plotter — marks position.
2. Throw flotation (horseshoe, dan buoy) immediately in their direction.
3. Point: designate one person to do nothing but point at the MOB continuously.
4. Recovery manoeuvre: Williamson turn or quick-stop — on a cruiser under sail, the easiest in flat water is to gybe round onto a reciprocal and approach slowly into the wind with sails depowered.
5. Make the approach from downwind so the boat drifts onto them, not over them. Stop the engine in neutral before they're near the prop.
6. Recovery is hard. Consider using the swim platform, or a halyard + bowline loop as a hoist.

**Cold reality**: recovering a limp adult from the water is extremely difficult short-handed. Prevention > cure. Tether up at night and in weather.

### Distress Calling

**MAYDAY** — grave and imminent danger to life.

```
MAYDAY MAYDAY MAYDAY
This is [YACHT NAME], [YACHT NAME], [YACHT NAME]
MMSI [your 9-digit number]
Position: [lat/long or bearing and distance from landmark]
Nature of distress: [sinking / on fire / man overboard / serious medical]
Number of persons on board: [N]
I require immediate assistance.
Over.
```

**PAN-PAN** — urgent, not life-threatening (broken mast, lost steering in safe water).

DSC distress (red button under a flap on the VHF) sends the above digitally — use it first, then follow with voice on 16.

**Greek Coast Guard** (Λιμενικό): VHF 16, call "Olympia Radio" or the local port. Shore phone: **108** (Greek coastguard emergency number).

## 13. Pre-departure Checklist

### At the charter base (handover, ~2 hours)

- Hull inspection (scratches, dings — photograph everything).
- Engine compartment: belts, hoses, oil, coolant, raw-water strainer.
- Bilge dry; bilge pump cycles manually.
- Seacocks identified and operated (every single one — stiff one today = flooded boat next week).
- Batteries charged; voltage 12.6+ on both banks at rest.
- Fuel tank: full and verified (dip the tank if possible).
- Water tanks: full.
- Gas bottle: full, spare stowed, solenoid works.
- Heads: all flush, Y-valves set correctly.
- Running rigging: every line identified, clutches work, winches spin freely.
- Sails: unfurl both in the marina, look for tears, check furlers roll cleanly.
- Anchor: deploy 10 m, test windlass up and down, foot switches and helm remote both.
- Electronics: plotter boots, GPS lock, depth transducer reads, wind instrument rotates, AIS sees traffic, VHF radio check with base on ch 72.
- Navigation lights: all work.
- Safety kit: inventory present, liferaft in date, flares in date.
- Dinghy + outboard: engine starts, oars on board, fuel.
- Documents: charter agreement, crew list, insurance certificate, VHF licence (skipper), transit log, ship's papers.

### Daily, before casting off

- Weather checked (2 sources).
- Route plugged in the plotter with waypoints.
- Crew briefed: destination, ETA, tacks/gybes likely, who does what at the dock.
- Lifejackets accessible.
- Hatches and ports closed (this surprises new crew every time).
- Engine checks done (see §4).
- Lines and fenders sorted for departure manoeuvre.
- Dinghy raised on davits or towed short.
- Hot stove / kettle off.

### Daily, before dropping anchor for the night

- Holding confirmed with transit.
- Anchor alarm set.
- Nav lights tested if staying after dark.
- Tender secured (tied long, or raised).
- Weather re-checked — overnight shifts?
- Bilge checked dry.

### Final thought

The Ionian is the easiest cruising ground in the Mediterranean: reliable thermal winds, flat water in the lee of the islands, short hops, warm water, and villages with quays that expect you. A 2019 Bavaria C45 is a forgiving, stable boat that will teach you more than it frustrates you.

Two things separate confident skippers from stressed ones here:

1. **Be the first boat into a harbour, not the last.** 14:30 arrivals have their pick of berths; 18:00 arrivals are rafting up to a fishing boat in cross-wind.
2. **Treat the manoeuvre like a pilot treats a landing** — briefed, planned, rehearsed. Every crew member knows their job before you're committed.

Fair winds.
